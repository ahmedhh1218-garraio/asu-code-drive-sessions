◆ Concept

The GPIO can't do it alone

◆ Words first

Five words before we wire anything:

GPIO
General-Purpose Input/Output — a pin your program can switch HIGH or LOW. A tiny software-controlled light switch.
H-bridge
A circuit of four switches that can send current through a motor either direction. Named for its “H” shape.
Motor driver (L298N)
The chip containing the H-bridge; it takes weak logic signals and switches the strong motor power. A power amplifier for movement.
PWM (duty cycle)
Switching a pin on/off very fast; the % of time it's on (duty cycle) sets the speed. Flicking a light to “dim” it.
Common ground
Connecting the 0 V of two power sources so they share a reference. Without it, signals mean nothing.

⚡ Power gap

A Pi pin sources ~16 mA at 3.3 V. A motor pulls hundreds of mA at 6–12 V. Connect them directly and you fry the pin.

🔀 Direction

To reverse a motor you must swap which terminal is + and which is −. An H-bridge does that with four switches.

🛡️ Isolation

The L298N keeps the noisy, high-current motor side away from your delicate logic side.

◆ The control logic

Two pins, four behaviours

Each motor has IN1 and IN2. Their combination sets the behaviour; the ENable pin (with PWM) sets the speed.

◆ Deep dive

Inside the H-bridge: four switches in an "H"

The name comes from the shape: two pairs of transistor switches with the motor across the middle bar. Which diagonal you close decides which way current — and the motor — flows.

Why never close one side's top & bottom together

If Q1 and Q3 are on at once, you connect +V straight to GND through the transistors — a dead short called shoot-through. It dumps huge current and can destroy the chip in milliseconds. That's exactly why IN1=HIGH, IN2=HIGH is used only briefly as an active brake, never as a drive state.

Flyback / back-EMF

A motor is a coil; when you switch it off, its magnetic field collapses and shoves current backward. Protection diodes (built into the L298N) give that surge a safe path. Without them, the spike fries transistors. This is also why braking hard can momentarily generate voltage.

The L298N tax: it's an old bipolar design that drops ~1.5–2 V and runs hot. Modern MOSFET drivers (TB6612FNG, DRV8833) waste far less. Good to know for a competition where every volt of speed counts.

◆ Deep dive

PWM: fake an analog voltage with a digital pin

A GPIO is only ON or OFF. Flip it thousands of times per second and the motor "feels" the average. Duty cycle = fraction of time it's ON. The bar below animates your current throttle.

0 V effective~7.2 V12 V effective
Higher duty = higher average voltage = faster motor. Too low and the motor buzzes but won't turn (it can't overcome friction). That minimum is your car's dead-band.
◆ Deep dive

Frequency matters too — and where it comes from

Two independent knobs

Duty cycle sets how fast (the average voltage). Frequency sets how often the cycle repeats. Too low a frequency (<100 Hz) and the motor jerks and whines audibly; a few hundred Hz to low kHz is the sweet spot for small DC motors.

duty → speedfrequency → smoothness

Hardware vs software PWM

Hardware PWM is generated by a dedicated timer peripheral — rock-steady, zero CPU. But a board has only a few HW-PWM pins. Software PWM toggles a normal GPIO from a loop or thread; flexible, but the non-real-time scheduler adds jitter that you hear as a wavering whine.

HW = steadySW = jittery

What our car actually does

The GPIO backend picks hardware PWM on the enable pins when the board offers it, and falls back to a software-PWM thread (via libgpiod) otherwise. That's a direct consequence of Session 2's lesson: the same high-level "set speed 60%" is implemented differently depending on what the driver + board expose — the app doesn't care which.

◆ Kinematics

How two motors make a turn

Our car has no steering wheel. It steers like a tank: spin the left and right wheels at different speeds/directions. This is differential (skid) drive.

Forward+full+fullStraight ahead
Gentle left+half+fullCurves left
Spin left−full+fullRotates in place
Stop00Coast to rest
Sharp spin turns (wheels opposite) are precise but scrub the tyres and can confuse the IMU; gentle curve turns (one wheel slower) are smoother. Your AI will learn which the human demonstrator preferred.
◆ From command to pins

The code that moves metal

Everything above collapses into a small, readable driver. The dashboard sends a word; this maps it to pin states and a PWM duty.

# car_control.py (simplified)
def forward(self, speed):
    self.gpio.set(IN1, HIGH); self.gpio.set(IN2, LOW)   # left motor fwd
    self.gpio.set(IN3, HIGH); self.gpio.set(IN4, LOW)   # right motor fwd
    self.pwm(ENA, speed);     self.pwm(ENB, speed)      # duty 0..100

def turn_left(self, speed):
    self.gpio.set(IN1, LOW);  self.gpio.set(IN2, HIGH)  # left motor reverse
    self.gpio.set(IN3, HIGH); self.gpio.set(IN4, LOW)   # right motor fwd → spin left
    self.pwm(ENA, speed);     self.pwm(ENB, speed)

def stop(self):
    for pin in (IN1, IN2, IN3, IN4): self.gpio.set(pin, LOW)  # coast
Notice: the AI model never learns pin numbers. It outputs one of forward / left / right / stop, and this layer turns that into voltages. Clean separation = easy to port to another board.
◆ Hands-on lab · ~60 min

Wire it & tune it

  1. Wire the L298N. Motor supply to +12V/GND, the two motors to OUT1-2 and OUT3-4, and IN1..IN4 + ENA/ENB to your GPIO pins.
  2. Share a ground. Connect the L298N GND to the board GND. Without a common ground the logic signals mean nothing — the single most common wiring mistake.
  3. Test each direction from the dashboard: forward, back, left, right, stop. Confirm both wheels spin the expected way (swap a motor's two wires if one is reversed).
  4. Find the dead-band. Lower the throttle until a wheel stops turning. That percentage is your minimum useful duty — set MIN_DUTY just above it.
  5. Match the motors. If the car drifts when told to go straight, one motor is stronger. Add a small trim to the weaker side until it tracks straight.
Brown-out warning: never power the motors from the Pi's 5 V pin. A motor's inrush current dips the rail and reboots the board mid-drive. Use a separate battery for the motor supply, joined only at ground.

🏭 From the field

Sensors lie — filters are the professional answer

No sensor gives you the truth. You get a noisy, sometimes-wrong number, and the professional discipline is deciding what to believe: median-of-N, low-pass, dead-band, sanity limits. Come with a preferred filter and be ready to defend it.
